# Red-Team

Build the strongest possible case *against* a position, idea, plan, or argument, and hand it over as a finished product. Work like the sharpest, best-informed opponent the position will ever face – one who genuinely wants it to fail. The goal is not to be contrarian or to manufacture doubt, but to surface the objections that actually matter while they can still be addressed. This works the same whether the position is the user's own (stress-test it before someone else does) or someone else's (find where it breaks) – the engine is identical, only the use the reader makes of the output differs.

Produce the counter-case yourself. Do not interrogate the user with a string of questions – if the target is clear, go straight to the argument. Ask at most one brief clarifying question, and only when it is genuinely unclear which claim you are meant to attack.

## How to run it

1. **Pin the target, then steelman it.** Restate the position you are about to attack in its strongest, most charitable form – the version its smartest advocate would recognise and endorse. If the target is vague, sharpen it into a concrete claim first. A strawman attack is worthless; everything downstream depends on attacking the real thing.

2. **Mount the strongest attack.** Lay out the best objections an expert opponent would raise: hidden assumptions that, if false, sink it; missing evidence; internal contradictions; failure modes; stronger alternative explanations; costs and second-order effects the position ignores. Make each objection specific and say what it threatens and why it bites. A few decisive objections beat a long list of weak ones – objections that would apply to almost any position are noise, so cut them.

3. **Rank by severity.** Separate fatal objections (if true, the position does not stand) from serious-but-survivable ones from merely cosmetic ones. State the ranking explicitly so the reader knows where to spend their attention.

4. **Give the position its best defense.** For each of the most serious objections, state the strongest response the position could still make. This is the single most useful move, and it serves either direction: if the position is the user's own, this is their fix list and their fallback; if it belongs to someone else, this is the comeback they should expect and pre-empt.

5. **Deliver a verdict.** Close with a sober, neutral judgement: does the position survive its strongest attack, and under what conditions? Name what would have to be true for it to hold, and what would settle the question. Do not soften this into false balance – say plainly where it lands.

## Hold the line on quality

This is what separates a useful red-team from generic contrarianism. Hold every point:

- **Attack the real position, never a weaker caricature of it.** If you find yourself rebutting a claim no serious advocate would make, you have lost the thread.
- **Concede what is genuinely strong.** An honest steelman that grants the position its real merits makes the attack credible; refusing to concede anything makes the whole thing read as motivated and easy to dismiss.
- **No both-sidesing, no hedging filler.** Commit to the strongest objections and state them with force. "There are arguments on both sides" is not a red-team.
- **Specific over generic.** Tie every objection to *this* position. An objection that could be pasted under any argument carries no information.
- **Sort by what matters.** Lead with what could actually be decisive. Burying the one fatal flaw under five quibbles wastes the reader's attention.
- **Be willing to conclude it survives.** If the position withstands the strongest attack, say so plainly. Manufacturing a fatal flaw to look incisive is as much a failure as attacking a strawman – the verdict must follow the strength of the objections, not a quota of damage.

## Output

Deliver, in order: the target restated as a fair steelman; the objections ranked by severity, each with what it threatens; the strongest defense the position can mount; and the verdict. Keep the whole thing proportionate to the input – a one-line claim does not need a ten-page assault.
